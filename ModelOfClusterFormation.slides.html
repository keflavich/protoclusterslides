Why are (high-mass) star clusters important?

  • Only locations in which a (probably coeval) IMF is fully sampled
  • Most luminous sub-regions of a galaxy
    • visible as point-sources in nearby galaxies (& therefore countable!)
  • Most massive gravitationally bound objects forming in the \(z=0\) universe?
    • formation conditions may be representative of early universe
    • low-mass analogs to Globular clusters

A simple model of star cluster populations

-Where can they form?

-What is their mass function?

-How do they progress from gas to stars?

Where do star clusters form?

Two simple models:

  • Uniform disk:

\(p(r) = 1\) if \(r_1 < r < r_2\)

\(p(r) = 0\) otherwise

  • Exponential disk:

\(p(r) = r_1 e^{-r}\) for \(r>r_1\)

\(p(r) = 0\) for \(r<r_1\)

What is their mass function?

A Schecter function? Or a power-law?

The intrinsic mass function: \[p(m) = A m^{-\beta} e^{-m/m_0} \] where \(m_0\) is a "maximum mass" above which the number of clusters exponentially drops off

How do the massive clusters form?

  • Either all their gas is in place at once
    • call this the "prestellar core" model
  • Or it isn't
    • Some stars form, then others are accreted?
    • Or, some stars form, and gas is accreted
    • implies multiple generations
    • or "prolonged" star formation

Start with the first model: We can constrain it.

  • First, clusters go through a "starless" phase
  • Then, they form stars!
  • Finally, the stars expel the gas

The starry, gas-rich phase lasts at least until the first supernova if \(v_{esc} > C_{II}\) (Bressert, Ginsburg et al 2012) or until the gas is consumed

The first SN is at \(t>3\) Myr. The gas consumption timescale may be shorted.

We measure an upper limit:

\(N_{starless}(M>10^4) = 0\)

\(N_{starry}(M>10^4) = 3\) (conservatively) or \(18\) (if you allow for 100% star formation efficiency)

The Urquhart et al 2013 ATLASGAL sample extends this to ~6 in the conservative case.

  • By selecting on masers, that sample may be biased
  • Contreras and Longmore working on the unbiased sample?

So we "measure" \(N_{starless}/N_{starry}\) = 0.

Poisson statistics: \[\sigma_N = \frac{\sqrt{N_{starry}}}{N_{starry}} \approx 0.6 \]

Or, a \(1-\sigma\) limit \(N_{starless} < 0.6\)

Lifetime of the starless phase:

\[\frac{N_{starless}}{N_{starry}} = \frac{\tau_{starless}}{\tau_{starry}}\]

\[ \tau_{starless} < \tau_{starry} \frac{\sqrt{N_{starry}}}{N_{starry}} = 3 Myr * \frac{0.6}{3} = 0.6 Myr\]

Better statistics aren't warranted yet.

T = astropy.table.Table.read('BGPS_vlsr.cat',format='ascii')
v = T['col7']
fk5 = astropy.coordinates.ICRS(T['col2']*u.deg, T['col3']*u.deg)
lon,lat = fk5.galactic.l.to(u.deg).value, fk5.galactic.b.to(u.deg).value
mask = (v<999)
v = v[mask]
lon = lon[mask]
lat = lat[mask]
dmax = agpy.vector_kdist(lon,lat,v,near=False,silent=True)
bgps = astropy.table.Table.read('bolocam_gps_v1_0_1.tbl',format='ascii.ipac')
f40 = np.array([bgps['flux_40'][bgps['cnum'] == x] for x in T['col1'][mask]]).squeeze()
# Use standard conversion factors assuming T=20K, 1.5x beam correction & 1.5x flux correction, kappa = 0.0114 cm^2/g
mmax = 14.01 * f40 * 2.25 * (dmax/1e3)**2
